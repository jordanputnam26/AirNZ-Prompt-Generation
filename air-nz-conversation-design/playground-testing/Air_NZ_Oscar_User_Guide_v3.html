How we will evaluate Oscar — the four steps

An overview before we go into detail
How we will evaluate Oscar

Evaluation involves the four steps below. Each step is covered in detail on the pages that follow. Before you begin, read through this overview so you have the shape of the process in your head before you open the playground. None of the steps are long. Most of the value comes from choosing the right test mode, picking a clear observation lens, and writing a precise remark at the end.

1Choose your test mode
Decide whether you are running a structured service-contract test or a persona-led conversation test. Both are valid. The structured mode is best for checking rewrite fidelity against supplied inputs. The persona-led mode is best for checking register, pacing, and conversational feel.
2Choose your lens and checkpoint
Pick one persona lens, either Nīkau or Wei, then choose one or two checkpoints from the checklist. The persona tells you what kind of customer you are pressure-testing for. The checkpoint tells you what to observe.
3Run the test in the playground
For structured tests, paste the supplied Latest Customer Message and Service Contract input. For persona-led tests, start a fresh chat and send a natural opening in character. In both cases, test one thing on purpose rather than everything at once.
4Record your feedback on the checklist
After the conversation, fill in the Pass / Fail / Notes column for the checkpoint(s) you tested. Quote the phrase, name the moment, and say whether Oscar changed the meaning, overstepped the contract, or simply sounded wrong. Remarks are the point, not the score.
What this guide is for. Use it to test whether Oscar responds the way the current prompt intends: calm, practical, clear, grounded, and appropriate to the Claim Missing Airpoints scenario. What it is not for. It is not a full technical QA pass of the production service-agent and rewriter pipeline. If the wording changed the meaning, softened the outcome too much, invented new detail, or overstepped the supplied contract, capture that in your remarks.

Freeform prompt · register and behaviour

Step 2 — Pick a checkpoint from the checklist

One or two checkpoints is plenty — your remarks are what we act on.

Alongside this guide, you have a separate editable document: the Evaluation Checklist. It contains twenty-eight observable checkpoints organised into nine sections (A through I). Each checkpoint is written to work in both test modes. Some focus on pure voice and behaviour. Some focus on rewrite fidelity and contract discipline. Together, they test whether Oscar is responding the way the current prompt intends.

Before you open the playground, pick one or two checkpoints you want to test. You do not need to test them all. Choose the ones most relevant to the mode you picked. In structured mode, this often means information accuracy, confirmation behaviour, bad news, or boundary handling. In persona-led mode, it often means tone, pacing, register adaptation, or closure.

A useful observation is specific. Quote the phrase. Name the moment. Say what you would have expected instead. If the wording felt polished but changed the meaning, dropped a protected value, softened a hard outcome, or made Oscar sound like he had more authority than the contract allowed, that is important evidence.

Step 2 — continued
How to fill in the checklist
PASS, FAIL, or notes — whichever fits, with evidence.
If you’re sure → Pass or fail
Mark the checkpoint PASS or FAIL. Either way, give a reason. Quote the phrase, name the moment, describe exactly what you saw, and say why it matters.
“FAIL — Oscar added ‘you can call Customer Care if it still hasn’t landed’ even though the contract did not include a contact path. Helpful tone, but overstepped the supplied task.”
If you’re not sure → Notes
Don’t force a pass or fail. Use the Notes space to describe what didn’t feel right. This is useful when the response is almost right but still feels too soft, too robotic, too long, or slightly off-contract.
“Technically preserved the contract, but the confirmation prompt felt too wordy for Nīkau. It sounded careful rather than quick and capable.”
Remarks are the point. A PASS or FAIL on its own is just a score. The remark tells us why. The most useful remarks name the exact phrase, the checkpoint it missed, whether the meaning changed, whether Oscar overstepped the contract, and what a better Oscar response would have done instead.

Step 3 — Mode A: structured copy-paste tests

Use this path when you have a supplied Latest Customer Message and Service Contract.

This is the most production-like way to test Oscar. The point is not to judge whether the upstream service decision was correct. The point is to judge whether Oscar rewrites that supplied decision in the right voice without changing the meaning, dropping protected values, inventing new details, or overstepping the contract.

1
Start a fresh playground conversation with the current Oscar system prompt loaded
Open the playground, select the current Oscar build, and start a new conversation. You do not need hidden persona injection for this path. Your test input will carry the contract context in the message itself.
2
Paste the supplied test input exactly as written
Use the full block you were given, including Latest Customer Message and Service Contract. Do not paraphrase it first. The exact input matters because this path is testing rewrite fidelity as well as tone.
3
Check the response against the contract before you judge the style
First ask: did Oscar preserve the protected values, required concepts, and business outcome? Only then ask whether it sounds like Oscar. A polished but unfaithful response is still a fail.
What this path is testing. Structured tests are the best way to catch meaning drift, dropped protected values, softened outcomes, invented detail, and accidental overreach. They are also the best way to see whether Oscar can sound human and helpful without taking liberties with the supplied contract.

Watch for: minimal useful ask, no invented policy language, clean next step.

Use when: claim details have been collected and need confirmation.

Step 3 — continued
Mode B: persona-led conversation tests
Use this path when you want to feel Oscar in live dialogue.

In this path, you start a fresh conversation and speak as the customer yourself. Use Nīkau or Wei as your lens, choose one or two checkpoints, and shape the conversation so those checkpoints get exercised. The goal is not to write the perfect conversation. The goal is to create the conditions where Oscar either succeeds clearly or fails clearly.

Stay in the persona’s register. If you are testing Nīkau, keep it terse and mobile-like. If you are testing Wei, use shorter formal sentences and avoid idioms. Oscar can only adapt if you give him something real to adapt to.

If you’re testing…Try opening with…
Nīkau — brisk opening“missing points from last friday”
Watch the first line. Does Oscar get to the point without sounding cold?
Nīkau — frustration“already tried the form. still nothing.”
Watch whether Oscar acknowledges the failure without getting sticky or over-apologetic.
Nīkau — wrong identifierWhen Oscar asks for a booking reference, give him something unhelpful like a seat number. Watch the re-ask. Is it graceful and efficient?
Wei — clarity and reassurance“Hello. I think my Airpoints did not come to my account after my flight. Can you help me please?”
Watch whether Oscar stays plain and practical without sounding patronising.
Wei — jargon pressure-test“I have the ticket number but I am not sure what booking reference means.”
Watch whether Oscar explains cleanly, without airline shorthand.
Wei — bad news deliveryLater in the conversation, say it was a reward booking or the flight was outside the eligible window. Watch whether Oscar leads with the reason, not just the refusal.
Stay in the persona’s register. If you’re Nīkau, write tersely, lowercase, no full stops. If you’re Wei, write in formal short sentences without idioms. Oscar adapts to your register, so the test is only useful if you stay in character.

Step 4 — Record your feedback on the checklist

Back to the checklist — write what you saw.

Once the conversation feels done, go back to the editable Evaluation Checklist document and record what you saw for the checkpoint(s) you chose. This is the most important step — your remarks are what we act on when we refine Oscar’s system prompt.

1
Mark PASS, FAIL, or NOTES against the checkpoint(s) you tested
Mark the checkpoint and add a short reason: quote the phrase, name the moment, describe exactly what you saw. If you’re uncertain, use Notes rather than forcing a pass or fail. If the wording felt nice but changed the underlying meaning, dropped a protected value, or made Oscar overstep the supplied contract, write that down explicitly.
2
Save and share the checklist back with the squad
Duplicate or export the checklist with your name in the filename (for example Oscar_Checklist_2026-05-26_Lauren) and share it back via the squad Slack channel or email it to the Tomoro delivery lead. We’ll consolidate across testers and feed the patterns into the next prompt iteration.
3
Optional — come back and run a second checkpoint
If you have time, start a fresh conversation, switch to the other mode, or pick a different checkpoint and run it again. One structured test and one persona-led test is the ideal pairing for a 20–25 minute session.
Remarks are what matter. A PASS or FAIL on its own is just a score. The remark tells us why. The best remarks are concrete: the exact phrase, the checkpoint it missed, whether the meaning changed or the contract was broken, and what Oscar should have done instead.

Worked examples — one from each testing mode

Optional reference. You don’t need to replicate these.

The next pages show two worked examples as reference. The first is a structured contract test. The second is a persona-led conversation test. You do not need to replicate them. They are here to show what good evidence looks like and how the checklist should be used.

How to read a worked example. Each example includes the test setup, the response, and the observations that matter. Read them as a pattern for your own notes: what was being tested, what Oscar said, and why the result passed or failed. If you see a response that sounds polished but breaks the contract, that is a fail.

Note on the worked examples. These are idealised. In a real test, Oscar will sometimes pass and sometimes fail — that’s the point of the playground. Where Oscar fails in your conversation, capture it in the checklist with the verbatim phrase that failed. If the response felt polished but wrong, say that too. That level of specificity is what we act on.

Good Oscar response

Kia ora. Before I submit it, please confirm these details are right: booking reference ABC123, ticket number 0861234567890, flight NZ283 on 2025-02-14, Auckland to Sydney.

If yes, I’ll submit the claim. If no, tell me what needs changing.
